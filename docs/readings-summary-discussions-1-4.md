## Session 1 — Reading Summary
### *What Is Special Education Today?* (pp. 1–6)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 1, pp. 1–6

**Chapter focus:** What is special education today, and who does it serve?

Wehmeyer and Kurth open by noting that the current education system was built for a bygone economy — one that no longer exists — and that students can no longer be successful focusing only on core academic skills. They must learn to communicate, be creative, engage in critical thinking, solve problems, and self-determine learning (p. 1). This framing applies with equal, if not greater, force to students with disabilities, yet the field has been slow to shift.

The authors anchor the legal definition of special education in IDEA: *specially designed instruction* means adapting content, methodology, or delivery of instruction (a) to address the unique needs of the child resulting from the disability, and (b) to ensure access to the general curriculum (34 C.F.R. §300.39(a)(1)). Critically, they stress that special education is **not a place** — it is the delivery of specialized instruction or content. This distinction is foundational: a student does not "go to" special education; special education comes to the student in the least restrictive setting possible.

The chapter introduces **supplementary aids and services** as a parallel and equally important legal concept. These are aids, services, and other supports provided in regular education classes and other settings to enable children with disabilities to be educated with nondisabled children to the maximum extent appropriate (§300.42). Table 1.1 organizes supplementary aids across five domains: school buildings and facilities, classrooms and instructional spaces, instructional technology and equipment, instructional materials and content presentation, schedules and task modifications, and people (teacher, paraeducator, peer, and planning roles).

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- The shift from "special education as place" to "specially designed instruction delivered in inclusive settings" is where equity begins. A neurodivergent student, a multilingual learner, or a student with multiple intersecting identities does not need to be removed to receive adapted instruction — they need the instruction to adapt to them.
- Supplementary aids and services are not charity; they are legally required environmental modifications. For CLD and neurodivergent learners, this includes sensory accommodations, assistive technology, modified task schedules, and collaborative people supports.
- The question Wehmeyer and Kurth pose — "Who do we teach now, and what and where should we be teaching them?" — is a prompt to reconsider the default assumptions embedded in how tasks, tools, and environments are designed.

**Connection to Post 2:** The post's argument that inclusion is a design responsibility, not a placement decision, draws directly from this chapter's insistence that special education is instruction, not a location. When a Minecraft or AI task creates barriers for some students but not others, the problem is the design of the task — not the students who encounter it.

## Session 2 — Reading Summary
### *What Is Special Education Today?* (pp. 7–14)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 1, pp. 7–14

**Chapter focus:** The Least Restrictive Environment mandate, *Endrew F.*, and what "appropriate" education actually requires.

Pages 7–14 continue Chapter 1 with a close reading of the Least Restrictive Environment (LRE) mandate and what it actually requires — as opposed to how it has typically been misread. The LRE language in IDEA (§300.114(a)(2)) states that children with disabilities must be educated with children who are nondisabled to the maximum extent appropriate, and that removal occurs **only if** education in regular classes with supplementary aids and services **cannot be achieved satisfactorily**. Wehmeyer and Kurth argue that this has been persistently misread to mean that placement decisions should be driven by the nature and severity of a student's disability — when the language actually says the opposite: removal is justified only when the regular education environment fails the student *even with* full supplementary support.

The consequences of this misreading are stark: only 17% of students with intellectual disabilities and fewer than half of students with emotional and behavioral disabilities are educated in general education settings for 80% or more of their day (p. 8).

The second major argument in this section concerns *Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District* (2017). The U.S. Supreme Court ruled that the *Rowley* standard (requiring only "some benefit" from an IEP) was insufficient. Under *Endrew F.*, a child's educational program must be:
- **Appropriately ambitious** in light of the child's circumstances
- Designed to enable the child to meet **challenging objectives**
- Built from consideration of the child's present levels of achievement, disability, and **potential for growth**
- Tailored to the **unique circumstances** of the individual child (p. 12)

Rather than rely solely on present levels of achievement and the child's disability, the program must take into consideration the child's potential for growth and unique circumstances.

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- The *Endrew F.* standard makes ambition a legal requirement. For a neurodivergent student, a CLD learner, or a student whose full identity has been underestimated by deficit-model thinking, this standard demands that teachers and IEP teams start from a presumption of growth.
- "Appropriate" is not the same as "comfortable" or "convenient." Keeping a student in a segregated setting because it is easier to manage is not appropriate under IDEA's LRE mandate.
- The phrase "unique circumstances" opens space to include cultural context, language background, and the compounding effects of multiple identities — not just diagnostic labels — in designing a student's educational program.

**Connection to Post 2:** The post directly extends these arguments into technology-integrated classrooms. A Minecraft task that works smoothly for some students but creates invisible barriers for multilingual learners, neurodivergent students, or students with limited prior technology access is, in practice, delivering something less than an appropriately ambitious education to those students. *Endrew F.* raises the bar for what counts as adequate.

## Session 3 — Reading Summary
### *Can We Broaden Our Reach?* (pp. 15–20)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 2, pp. 15–20

**Chapter focus:** How we understand disability shapes what we do about it — from biomedical deficit models to social-ecological, person-environment fit models.

Chapter 2 asks why, nearly 50 years after the Education for All Handicapped Children Act, students with disabilities continue to be segregated and held to low expectations. The answer, Wehmeyer and Kurth argue, lies in the **model of disability** that education has implicitly inherited.

**Biomedical model:** Disability is located inside the person — it is pathology, deficit, impairment. The early institutions of special education were built on this model. Interventions were designed to treat, prevent, or reduce the damage of the disability. As the authors put it, the newly established special education system "inherited an understanding of disability as pathology and deficit and created an educational version of the medical model of services: separate and segregated classrooms and buildings where practitioners emphasized treatment, prevention, and reduction of damage and injury" (p. 17).

**Social model:** By the mid-20th century, disability rights advocates — many of them disabled people and their families — began demanding a different understanding. The disability rights movement argued that disability arises "from the discrimination and disadvantage individuals experience in relation to others because of their particular differences and characteristics" (Bach, 2017, p. 40, cited in Wehmeyer & Kurth, p. 19). Within a social model, the unit of analysis shifts from the individual to the social, legal, economic, and political structures that define value and status.

**Social-ecological / Person-environment fit model:** The WHO's 2001 International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (ICF) formalized a model that locates disability not in the person but in the **gap between what a person can do and what they want to do** (Buntinx, 2013, cited p. 18). Under this model, disability is essentially irrelevant when someone is fully participating in their environment with appropriate supports. The critical insight: *we cannot understand disability without taking into account people's strengths and abilities and the contexts in which people live, learn, work, and play* (p. 19).

The disability rights movement also connected disability to identity. Longmore and Umansky (2001) argued that disability has never been a monolithic grouping — it encompasses varied experiences of cultural devaluation, socially imposed restriction, and collective struggles for self-definition and self-determination that recur across disability groups and personal histories (p. 19).

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- For neurodivergent learners, the shift from biomedical to person-environment fit thinking means that executive-function differences, sensory sensitivities, or processing differences are not deficits to be fixed — they are characteristics that interact with environment. Change the environment, change the disability experience.
- For CLD learners, a social-ecological lens demands that we ask how language demands, cultural assumptions embedded in tasks, and implicit definitions of "normal" functioning create barriers that are then misattributed to the student.
- Disability identity and culture matter. Students who carry multiple identities — neurodivergent and multilingual, disabled and a person of color, or any intersection thereof — bring a range of experiences that do not fit neatly into any single diagnostic or demographic category.

**Connection to Post 3:** The post's core claim — that "stuck" looks the same on the surface whether the barrier is a login error, sensory overload, language load, or limited prior experience — is grounded in exactly this reading. When we apply a biomedical lens, all of those students look like they have a problem. When we apply a person-environment fit lens, we ask instead: what is the environment doing that is producing this barrier, and how do we change it?

## Session 4 — Reading Summary
### *Can We Broaden Our Reach?* (pp. 20–26)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 2, pp. 20–26

**Chapter focus:** From understanding disability differently to doing inclusion differently — strengths-based approaches, MTSS, UDL, and self-determination.

The second half of Chapter 2 turns from theory to practice. Having argued that disability is best understood through social-ecological, person-environment fit models, Wehmeyer and Kurth ask what this understanding changes about how education is actually structured.

**"Having" a disability — an outdated phrase:** The authors argue that as long as educators use the language of "having" a disability, they perpetuate biomedical, deficit-oriented thinking. In a social or person-environment fit model, the person does not *have* a disability — disability resides in the gap between what the person can do and what the environment demands (p. 20). Continuing to treat IDEA eligibility as a diagnostic property of the student (rather than as a description of a mismatch between person and environment) perpetuates a system of segregation and discrimination.

The solution, they argue, is to adopt practices that: focus on all children; are strengths-based; emphasize universal design; take a schoolwide approach to high-quality instruction; emphasize student agency and self-determined learning; abandon the notion of average as useful; and ensure personalized education for all children (p. 20).

**Strengths-Based Approaches:** Person-environment fit models require beginning with a student's strengths, abilities, and competencies rather than their deficits. There is "considerable power in beginning with a student's strengths, among them that it can enhance a positive relationship between the student and the teacher" (p. 21). In strengths-based approaches, supports are defined as anything that can enhance functioning and self-determination to enable a person to be successful.

**Schoolwide Approaches (MTSS):** The authors describe Multitiered Systems of Supports (MTSS) as the schoolwide embodiment of person-environment fit thinking. In MTSS, what changes when students are struggling is not where students are educated but the **type, intensity, or duration of instruction** (p. 22). Tier 1 instruction is high-quality, evidence-based, and universally designed — taking into account linguistic and cultural backgrounds, abilities, and other learning support needs. Tier 2 adjusts the instruction in some way without removing the student. Tier 3 provides individualized, intensive supports — still in general education settings.

**Universal Design for Learning (UDL):** UDL is defined as "the design of instructional materials and activities that allows the learning goals to be achievable by individuals with wide differences in their abilities to see, hear, speak, move, read, write, understand English, attend, organize, engage, and remember" (Orkwis & McLane, 1998, p. 9, cited p. 23). UDL is a form of supplementary aid — it designs the material itself to be accessible, rather than retrofitting access after the fact. Universally designed materials benefit all students, not just students with disabilities.

**Self-Determination:** Strengths-based approaches place the student at the center of all learning (p. 24). Self-determination became a focus in special education when the first students served under EHA began exiting school to less positive outcomes than expected. Evidence over three decades shows that enhanced self-determination leads to better school performance and transition-to-adulthood outcomes (Shogren & Wehmeyer, 2020, cited p. 24). In personalized education, students must become owners of their own learning — able to identify what they do well, what they are interested in, and how to chart their own path.

The chapter closes by connecting these frameworks to the *Endrew F.* standard: achieving an appropriately ambitious education based on potential for growth is impossible as long as deficit-focused practices remain in place. When the WHO ICF model defines disability as the impact on full participation, it means that a student who is fully participating — even with supports — is, in practice, not disabled in any educationally meaningful sense (p. 25).

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- For neurodivergent learners: MTSS and UDL are not remedial accommodations — they are schoolwide design principles that presume variability as the norm, not the exception. A launch routine that gives all students multiple entry points into a task is not doing something extra for neurodivergent students; it is designing for the full range of learners in any typical classroom.
- For CLD learners: UDL's explicit inclusion of "understand English" as one dimension of human variability affirms that language access is a design problem, not a student deficiency. Tier 1 instruction that does not account for linguistic and cultural background is not actually Tier 1 for multilingual students.
- For students with multiple identities: Self-determination is not a skill that belongs only to some students. It is a right — and denying agency to neurodivergent, CLD, or otherwise multiply marginalized students by designing tasks that do not invite their participation as thinkers and decision-makers is an equity issue.
- The phrase "disability is essentially irrelevant" when a person is fully participating (p. 25) is a radical reframe. It shifts the teacher's question from "How disabled is this student?" to "What supports and designs will make full participation possible?"

**Connection to Post 4:** This reading frames Discussion 4 around three interconnected moves: starting from strengths rather than deficits, designing environments that make full participation possible for the whole range of students in the room, and treating student agency and self-determination not as rewards for compliant behavior but as the structural goal of inclusive instruction. For a neurodivergent student, a multilingual learner, or a student navigating multiple marginalized identities, this means the teacher's first question about any task is: does this design make it possible for this student to show their thinking and make choices about their learning?
