## Session 1 — Reading Summary
### *What Is Special Education Today?* (pp. 1–6)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 1, pp. 1–6

**Chapter focus:** What is special education today, and who does it serve?

Wehmeyer and Kurth open by noting that the current education system was built for a bygone economy — one that no longer exists — and that students can no longer be successful focusing only on core academic skills. They must learn to communicate, be creative, engage in critical thinking, solve problems, and self-determine learning (p. 1). This framing applies with equal, if not greater, force to students with disabilities, yet the field has been slow to shift.

The authors anchor the legal definition of special education in IDEA: *specially designed instruction* means adapting content, methodology, or delivery of instruction (a) to address the unique needs of the child resulting from the disability, and (b) to ensure access to the general curriculum (34 C.F.R. §300.39(a)(1)). Critically, they stress that special education is **not a place** — it is the delivery of specialized instruction or content. This distinction is foundational: a student does not “go to” special education; special education comes to the student in the least restrictive setting possible.

The chapter introduces **supplementary aids and services** as a parallel and equally important legal concept. These are aids, services, and other supports provided in regular education classes and other settings to enable children with disabilities to be educated with nondisabled children to the maximum extent appropriate (§300.42). Table 1.1 organizes supplementary aids across five domains: school buildings and facilities, classrooms and instructional spaces, instructional technology and equipment, instructional materials and content presentation, schedules and task modifications, and people (teacher, paraeducator, peer, and planning roles).

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- The shift from “special education as place” to “specially designed instruction delivered in inclusive settings” is where equity begins. A neurodivergent student, a multilingual learner, or a student with multiple intersecting identities does not need to be removed to receive adapted instruction — they need the instruction to adapt to them.
- Supplementary aids and services are not charity; they are legally required environmental modifications. For CLD and neurodivergent learners, this includes sensory accommodations, assistive technology, modified task schedules, and collaborative people supports.
- The question Wehmeyer and Kurth pose — “Who do we teach now, and what and where should we be teaching them?” — is a prompt to reconsider the default assumptions embedded in how tasks, tools, and environments are designed.

**Connection to Post 2:** The post’s argument that inclusion is a design responsibility, not a placement decision, draws directly from this chapter’s insistence that special education is instruction, not a location. When a task creates barriers for some students but not others, the problem is the design of the task — not the students who encounter it.

## Session 2 — Reading Summary
### *What Is Special Education Today?* (pp. 7–14)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 1, pp. 7–14

**Chapter focus:** The Least Restrictive Environment mandate, *Endrew F.*, and what “appropriate” education actually requires.

Pages 7–14 continue Chapter 1 with a close reading of the Least Restrictive Environment (LRE) mandate and what it actually requires — as opposed to how it has typically been misread. The LRE language in IDEA (§300.114(a)(2)) states that children with disabilities must be educated with children who are nondisabled to the maximum extent appropriate, and that removal occurs **only if** education in regular classes with supplementary aids and services **cannot be achieved satisfactorily**. Wehmeyer and Kurth argue that this has been persistently misread to mean that placement decisions should be driven by the nature and severity of a student’s disability — when the language actually says the opposite: removal is justified only when the regular education environment fails the student *even with* full supplementary support.

The second major argument concerns *Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District* (2017). Under *Endrew F.*, a child’s educational program must be appropriately ambitious in light of the child’s circumstances, designed to enable the child to meet challenging objectives, and built from consideration of the child’s present levels of achievement, disability, and **potential for growth** (p. 12).

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- The *Endrew F.* standard makes ambition a legal requirement. For a neurodivergent student, a CLD learner, or a student whose full identity has been underestimated by deficit-model thinking, this standard demands that teachers and IEP teams start from a presumption of growth.
- “Appropriate” is not the same as “comfortable” or “convenient.”
- The phrase “unique circumstances” opens space to include cultural context, language background, and the compounding effects of multiple identities — not just diagnostic labels — in designing a student’s educational program.

**Connection to Post 2:** A task that works smoothly for some students but creates invisible barriers for multilingual learners, neurodivergent students, or students with limited prior technology access is, in practice, delivering something less than an appropriately ambitious education to those students.

## Session 3 — Reading Summary
### *Can We Broaden Our Reach?* (pp. 15–20)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 2, pp. 15–20

**Chapter focus:** How we understand disability shapes what we do about it — from biomedical deficit models to social-ecological, person-environment fit models.

Chapter 2 asks why, nearly 50 years after the Education for All Handicapped Children Act, students with disabilities continue to be segregated and held to low expectations. The answer lies in the **model of disability** that education has implicitly inherited.

- **Biomedical model:** Disability is located inside the person — pathology, deficit, impairment. Special education inherited this model and built separate, segregated classrooms where practitioners emphasized treatment and reduction of damage (p. 17).
- **Social model:** Disability arises from the discrimination and disadvantage individuals experience in relation to others because of their particular differences and characteristics (Bach, 2017, cited p. 19). The unit of analysis shifts from the individual to the structures that define value and status.
- **Person-environment fit model:** The WHO’s ICF (2001) locates disability in the **gap between what a person can do and what they want to do** (p. 18). Disability is essentially irrelevant when someone is fully participating in their environment with appropriate supports.

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- For neurodivergent learners: executive-function differences, sensory sensitivities, and processing differences are not deficits to be fixed — they are characteristics that interact with environment. Change the environment, change the disability experience.
- For CLD learners: a social-ecological lens demands asking how language demands, cultural assumptions, and implicit definitions of “normal” functioning create barriers misattributed to the student.
- Disability identity and culture matter. Students carrying multiple identities bring a range of experiences that do not fit neatly into any single diagnostic or demographic category (Longmore & Umansky, 2001, cited p. 19).

**Connection to Post 3:** “Stuck” looks the same on the surface whether the barrier is a login error, sensory overload, language load, or limited prior experience. A person-environment fit lens asks: what is the environment doing that is producing this barrier, and how do we change it?

## Session 4 — Reading Summary
### *Can We Broaden Our Reach?* (pp. 20–26)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 2, pp. 20–26

**Chapter focus:** From understanding disability differently to doing inclusion differently — strengths-based approaches, MTSS, UDL, and self-determination.

The second half of Chapter 2 turns from theory to practice. Wehmeyer and Kurth argue that as long as educators use the language of “having” a disability, they perpetuate biomedical, deficit-oriented thinking. The solution is to adopt practices that: focus on all children; are strengths-based; emphasize universal design; take a schoolwide approach to high-quality instruction; emphasize student agency and self-determined learning; abandon the notion of average as useful; and ensure personalized education for all children (p. 20).

- **Strengths-based approaches:** Begin with a student’s strengths, abilities, and competencies rather than deficits. Supports are defined as anything that can enhance functioning and self-determination to enable a person to be successful (p. 21).
- **MTSS:** What changes when students are struggling is not where they are educated but the **type, intensity, or duration of instruction** (p. 22). Tier 1 instruction is universally designed, accounting for linguistic and cultural backgrounds, abilities, and other learning support needs.
- **UDL:** The design of instructional materials and activities that allows learning goals to be achievable by individuals with wide differences in abilities to see, hear, speak, move, read, write, understand English, attend, organize, engage, and remember (Orkwis & McLane, 1998, cited p. 23).
- **Self-determination:** Strengths-based approaches place the student at the center. Three decades of evidence shows that enhanced self-determination leads to better school performance and transition-to-adulthood outcomes (Shogren & Wehmeyer, 2020, cited p. 24).

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- UDL’s explicit inclusion of “understand English” as a dimension of human variability affirms that language access is a design problem, not a student deficiency.
- Self-determination is not a reward for finishing. It is what we are supposed to be designing toward.
- The phrase “disability is essentially irrelevant” when a person is fully participating (p. 25) shifts the teacher’s question from “How disabled is this student?” to “What supports and designs will make full participation possible?”

**Connection to Post 4:** The redesign phase of the Lego sail car lesson — the part most tied to student agency and self-determination — was treated as optional and was the first thing cut. That is the exact failure mode this chapter names: task design can signal strengths-based values without actually protecting the conditions that make student agency possible.

## Session 5 — Reading Summary
### *Self-Determination and Students with Autism Spectrum Disorders*
**Source:** Wehmeyer, M. L., & Shogren, K. A. (2013). In: *Handbook of Autism and Pervasive Developmental Disorders.* Wiley.

**Chapter focus:** What self-determination actually is, how it develops, and what teachers can do to promote it — with particular attention to students whose identities and neurotypes are most at risk of having their agency designed away.

### What Self-Determination Is

Wehmeyer and Shogren define self-determination as acting as the **causal agent** in one’s own life — making choices and decisions about one’s quality of life free from undue external influence or interference (Wehmeyer, 1992). The key word is *causal*: self-determined people make things happen in their lives rather than having things happen to them. This is not a personality trait or a developmental milestone that some students reach and others do not — it is a set of component skills that can be taught and that all students, regardless of disability label or support need, are capable of developing.

The functional model of self-determination identifies four essential characteristics of self-determined behavior:
1. **Autonomy** — acting according to one’s own preferences, interests, and abilities
2. **Self-regulation** — using strategies to manage behavior, set goals, and solve problems
3. **Psychological empowerment** — believing that one’s actions can influence outcomes
4. **Self-realization** — knowing oneself well enough to use that knowledge to advantage

These characteristics are developed through experience — specifically through opportunities to make choices, act on those choices, experience consequences, and reflect. Students who are chronically denied choices, who are over-supported in ways that transfer ownership to the adult, or whose learning environments do not invite self-reflection are systematically under-developing self-determination regardless of their diagnosis.

### Why It Is at Risk for Neurodivergent, CLD, and Multiply Identified Students

Wehmeyer and Shogren argue that the students most at risk of underdeveloped self-determination are those most likely to be in tightly managed, adult-directed environments — which disproportionately includes students with autism spectrum disorders, intellectual disabilities, and significant support needs. But the same logic applies to any student whose identity, language, or learning profile leads teachers to assume incompetence: students who are English language learners and also flagged for special education evaluation; students whose neurodivergence intersects with race, class, or immigration status; students who have been moved through a succession of placements that gave them little say in the matter.

The problem is not malicious. It is structural. Adults who want to help tend to take over. Systems designed around compliance tend to reward passivity. Schools that sort students by what they cannot do tend to design learning around those deficits. Every one of these conditions reduces the student’s opportunity to act as the causal agent in their own learning — and therefore slows or prevents the development of self-determination.

### What Teachers Can Do: Component Skills and Instructional Strategies

Wehmeyer and Shogren identify a set of **component skills** that together produce self-determined behavior. These include:
- Choice-making
- Decision-making
- Problem-solving
- Goal-setting and attainment
- Self-advocacy
- Self-awareness and self-knowledge
- Independence, risk-taking, and safety

Instructional approaches that build these skills include:
- **The Self-Determined Learning Model of Instruction (SDLMI):** A three-phase model in which students set a goal, take action, and adjust their plan — with the teacher acting as a facilitator rather than a director. The SDLMI works across disability categories and grade levels and has strong evidence for improving academic and transition outcomes.
- **Whose Future Is It Anyway?:** A student-directed transition planning curriculum that teaches students to lead their own IEP meetings, understand their rights, and make decisions about post-school goals.
- **Embedding choice throughout instruction:** Rather than treating choice as a reward (you get to choose once you finish), teachers can embed low-stakes choice points throughout a lesson — how to show what you know, which problem to start with, which tool to use, who to work with.

The research base is clear: students who are taught self-determination skills perform better academically, transition more successfully to adult life, and report higher quality of life (Shogren et al., 2015).

### Self-Determination as an Equity Issue

The chapter makes a point that is easy to miss: self-determination has historically been framed as a transition-planning concern, relevant mainly for older students approaching graduation. Wehmeyer and Shogren push back against this framing directly. Self-determination development begins in early childhood and depends on cumulative experience of choice, agency, and self-reflection across the entire K–12 span. Delaying attention to self-determination until high school means that the students who needed the most practice with agency have had twelve years of schooling that systematically denied it to them.

For our course theme, this is the equity argument at its sharpest. A neurodivergent student who has spent their entire school career in settings where adults direct, manage, and override their choices does not arrive at transition planning with underdeveloped self-determination because of their disability. They arrive with underdeveloped self-determination because their learning environments never treated their agency as the goal. The same is true for a CLD student whose language background has led teachers to over-scaffold in ways that remove decision-making from the student. The same is true for any student navigating multiple identities in a system that consistently reads their difference as deficit.

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- Self-determination is not a personality trait — it is a set of skills developed through cumulative experience of choice and agency. Teachers who design tasks without embedded choice points are not being neutral; they are actively shaping (and limiting) self-determination development.
- For neurodivergent students: the four characteristics of self-determined behavior (autonomy, self-regulation, psychological empowerment, self-realization) are not out of reach. They require teaching, opportunity, and environments that treat students as causal agents.
- For CLD students: over-scaffolding that removes decision-making from the student is a self-determination equity issue, not just a language support issue. The goal is not compliance with the task; it is the student’s ownership of their own learning.
- For students with multiple identities: the cumulative effect of adult-directed, compliance-oriented schooling across multiple years is the single biggest structural threat to self-determination development — regardless of disability label.
- The SDLMI framework (set a goal → take action → adjust the plan) is a practical classroom tool, not only a transition-planning tool. It can be embedded in any lesson where students make choices, take risks, and reflect on outcomes.

**Connection to Post 5:** Session 5’s reading deepens the argument started in Post 4. If self-determination is causal agency — making things happen in your own life — then the design question for every lesson is not just “Can all students access this task?” but “Does this task give all students the experience of being the one who decides?” For a neurodivergent student, a multilingual learner, or a student navigating multiple marginalized identities, that experience is not enrichment. It is the education they are legally and morally owed.
