## Session 1 — Reading Summary
### *What Is Special Education Today?* (pp. 1–6)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 1, pp. 1–6

**Chapter focus:** What is special education today, and who does it serve?

Wehmeyer and Kurth open by noting that the current education system was built for a bygone economy — one that no longer exists — and that students can no longer be successful focusing only on core academic skills. They must learn to communicate, be creative, engage in critical thinking, solve problems, and self-determine learning (p. 1). This framing applies with equal, if not greater, force to students with disabilities, yet the field has been slow to shift.

The authors anchor the legal definition of special education in IDEA: *specially designed instruction* means adapting content, methodology, or delivery of instruction (a) to address the unique needs of the child resulting from the disability, and (b) to ensure access to the general curriculum (34 C.F.R. §300.39(a)(1)). Critically, they stress that special education is **not a place** — it is the delivery of specialized instruction or content. This distinction is foundational: a student does not "go to" special education; special education comes to the student in the least restrictive setting possible.

The chapter introduces **supplementary aids and services** as a parallel and equally important legal concept. These are aids, services, and other supports provided in regular education classes and other settings to enable children with disabilities to be educated with nondisabled children to the maximum extent appropriate (§300.42). Table 1.1 organizes supplementary aids across five domains: school buildings and facilities, classrooms and instructional spaces, instructional technology and equipment, instructional materials and content presentation, schedules and task modifications, and people (teacher, paraeducator, peer, and planning roles).

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- The shift from "special education as place" to "specially designed instruction delivered in inclusive settings" is where equity begins. A neurodivergent student, a multilingual learner, or a student with multiple intersecting identities does not need to be removed to receive adapted instruction — they need the instruction to adapt to them.
- Supplementary aids and services are not charity; they are legally required environmental modifications. For CLD and neurodivergent learners, this includes sensory accommodations, assistive technology, modified task schedules, and collaborative people supports.
- The question Wehmeyer and Kurth pose — "Who do we teach now, and what and where should we be teaching them?" — is a prompt to reconsider the default assumptions embedded in how tasks, tools, and environments are designed.

**Connection to Post 2:** The post's argument that inclusion is a design responsibility, not a placement decision, draws directly from this chapter's insistence that special education is instruction, not a location. When a task creates barriers for some students but not others, the problem is the design of the task — not the students who encounter it.

## Session 2 — Reading Summary
### *What Is Special Education Today?* (pp. 7–14)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 1, pp. 7–14

**Chapter focus:** The Least Restrictive Environment mandate, *Endrew F.*, and what "appropriate" education actually requires.

Pages 7–14 continue Chapter 1 with a close reading of the Least Restrictive Environment (LRE) mandate and what it actually requires — as opposed to how it has typically been misread. The LRE language in IDEA (§300.114(a)(2)) states that children with disabilities must be educated with children who are nondisabled to the maximum extent appropriate, and that removal occurs **only if** education in regular classes with supplementary aids and services **cannot be achieved satisfactorily**. Wehmeyer and Kurth argue that this has been persistently misread to mean that placement decisions should be driven by the nature and severity of a student's disability — when the language actually says the opposite: removal is justified only when the regular education environment fails the student *even with* full supplementary support.

The consequences of this misreading are stark: only 17% of students with intellectual disabilities and fewer than half of students with emotional and behavioral disabilities are educated in general education settings for 80% or more of their day (p. 8).

The second major argument in this section concerns *Endrew F. v. Douglas County School District* (2017). The U.S. Supreme Court ruled that the *Rowley* standard (requiring only "some benefit" from an IEP) was insufficient. Under *Endrew F.*, a child's educational program must be appropriately ambitious in light of the child's circumstances, designed to enable the child to meet challenging objectives, built from consideration of the child's potential for growth, and tailored to the unique circumstances of the individual child (p. 12).

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- The *Endrew F.* standard makes ambition a legal requirement. For a neurodivergent student, a CLD learner, or a student whose full identity has been underestimated by deficit-model thinking, this standard demands that teachers and IEP teams start from a presumption of growth.
- "Appropriate" is not the same as "comfortable" or "convenient." Keeping a student in a segregated setting because it is easier to manage is not appropriate under IDEA's LRE mandate.
- The phrase "unique circumstances" opens space to include cultural context, language background, and the compounding effects of multiple identities — not just diagnostic labels.

**Connection to Post 2:** The post directly extends these arguments into technology-integrated classrooms. A task that works smoothly for some students but creates invisible barriers for multilingual learners, neurodivergent students, or students with limited prior technology access is, in practice, delivering something less than an appropriately ambitious education to those students.

## Session 3 — Reading Summary
### *Can We Broaden Our Reach?* (pp. 15–20)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 2, pp. 15–20

**Chapter focus:** How we understand disability shapes what we do about it — from biomedical deficit models to social-ecological, person-environment fit models.

Chapter 2 asks why, nearly 50 years after the Education for All Handicapped Children Act, students with disabilities continue to be segregated and held to low expectations. The answer lies in the **model of disability** that education has implicitly inherited. The biomedical model locates disability inside the person — as pathology, deficit, or impairment — while the social and social-ecological models locate disability in the gap between what a person can do and what the environment demands. The WHO ICF framework (2001) formalizes this: disability is essentially irrelevant when someone is fully participating in their environment with appropriate supports.

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- For neurodivergent learners, the shift from biomedical to person-environment fit thinking means that executive-function differences, sensory sensitivities, or processing differences are not deficits to be fixed — they are characteristics that interact with environment.
- For CLD learners, a social-ecological lens demands that we ask how language demands, cultural assumptions embedded in tasks, and implicit definitions of "normal" functioning create barriers that are then misattributed to the student.
- Disability identity and culture matter. Students who carry multiple identities — neurodivergent and multilingual, disabled and a person of color — bring a range of experiences that do not fit neatly into any single diagnostic or demographic category.

**Connection to Post 3:** The post's core claim — that "stuck" looks the same on the surface whether the barrier is a login error, sensory overload, language load, or limited prior experience — is grounded in this reading. A person-environment fit lens asks: what is the environment doing that is producing this barrier, and how do we change it?

## Session 4 — Reading Summary
### *Can We Broaden Our Reach?* (pp. 20–26)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 2, pp. 20–26

**Chapter focus:** From understanding disability differently to doing inclusion differently — strengths-based approaches, MTSS, UDL, and self-determination.

The second half of Chapter 2 turns from theory to practice. Wehmeyer and Kurth argue that as long as educators use the language of "having" a disability, they perpetuate deficit-oriented thinking. In a person-environment fit model, disability resides in the gap between what the person can do and what the environment demands — not in the person. The solution is to adopt practices that are strengths-based, universally designed, schoolwide, and self-determination-focused. MTSS operationalizes this at the school level: when students are struggling, what changes is the type, intensity, or duration of instruction — not where students are educated. UDL designs the material itself to be accessible from the start, and self-determination is framed not as a transition-planning milestone but as the structural goal of every classroom that treats students as owners of their learning.

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- For neurodivergent learners: MTSS and UDL presume variability as the norm, not the exception. A launch routine that gives all students multiple entry points is not doing something extra — it is designing for the full range.
- For CLD learners: UDL explicitly includes "understand English" as a dimension of human variability; language access is a design problem, not a student deficiency.
- For students with multiple identities: self-determination is a right, not a reward. Denying agency by designing tasks that do not invite participation as thinkers and decision-makers is an equity issue.

**Connection to Post 4:** This reading frames Post 4 around three moves: starting from strengths rather than deficits, designing environments for full participation across the whole range of students, and treating student agency not as a reward for compliance but as the structural goal of inclusive instruction.

## Session 5 — Reading Summary
### *Self-Determination as Causal Agency* (Wehmeyer & Shogren, 2013)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Shogren (2013), Self-determination and learners with autism spectrum disorder

**Chapter focus:** Self-determination is not a personality trait — it is the cumulative product of everyday experiences of choosing, acting, and adjusting.

Wehmeyer and Shogren define self-determined behavior through four characteristics: autonomy, self-regulation, psychological empowerment, and self-realization. Critically, they argue that these characteristics are not naturally developing traits — they are outcomes of environments that consistently position students as causal agents: the ones who set the direction, read results, and decide what comes next. Students who are chronically denied opportunities to choose and act do not arrive underprepared because of their disability, language background, or identity. They arrive underprepared because their learning environments never treated their agency as the goal.

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- For neurodivergent learners: self-determination develops through repeated, cumulative experience of real agency — not compliance, observation, or imitation. Classrooms that over-direct and over-scaffold neurodivergent students systematically deprive them of these formative experiences.
- For CLD learners: language-based over-scaffolding that removes student decision-making in the name of support actually suppresses the development of psychological empowerment and self-realization.
- For students with multiple identities: the debt owed to students who have been over-directed across years of schooling requires deliberately designing back in the experiences of causal agency they were denied.

**Connection to Post 5:** The post's central example — a student who stays quiet through setup instructions but comes alive when asked to put his own interest into an AI prompt, then unprompted adjusts the output when it is too complex — illustrates all four characteristics in real time. The task's key design move: agency was the entry point, not the reward for finishing.

## Session 6 — Reading Summary
### *How Can We Teach Better?* (pp. 41–46)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 3, pp. 41–46

**Chapter focus:** Designing effective inclusive supports through intentional scheduling, instructional planning, embedded instruction, and UDL-integrated technology.

In pages 41–46, Wehmeyer and Kurth focus on the structural and logistical decisions that determine whether students with disabilities — including neurodivergent, CLD, and multiply identified students — remain members of the classroom community or are quietly separated from it. Inclusive schedules should be built around general education routines first, then layered with co-teaching, paraprofessional support, and related services in ways that minimize pull-out and maximize time learning with peers. Instructional plans must link IEP goals to grade-level standards, prioritizing continued learning over "keeping up." The authors stress that "when to teach" should not become "where to teach": skill development and targeted goals should be embedded into natural classroom routines rather than isolated from peers through pull-out services that disrupt learning and account for hours of lost instructional time.

The section on technology and UDL argues that assistive and personal technologies are not stigmatizing extras — they are tools to be taught and embedded as normal features of the classroom. UDL is defined as designing instruction from the beginning to reduce barriers, and providing multiple formats (print, audio, simplified text with images) while holding all students to the same learning goals is a concrete way to honor variability in language, sensory needs, and processing profiles.

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- For neurodivergent learners: embedded instruction means that practice on IEP goals — including communication, regulation, and executive-function skills — happens inside general education routines rather than in separate spaces where instruction has already moved on.
- For CLD learners: multiple means of representation and technology-assisted learning reduce the language load on access without reducing the academic expectation. The goal remains the same; the route multiplies.
- For students with multiple identities: scheduling decisions, grouping structures, instructional layering, and format flexibility are equity decisions. They determine whether a student experiences belonging and sustained participation or subtle forms of separation within supposedly inclusive environments.

**Connection to Post 6:** The post's argument — that support should be proactive, normalized, and embedded in daily routines rather than reactive accommodations that appear only after students struggle — grows directly from this reading's insistence that "when to teach" must not default to "where to teach."

## Session 7 — Reading Summary
### *How Can We Teach Better?* (pp. 47–56)
**Source:** Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), Chapter 3, pp. 47–56

**Chapter focus:** Inclusive assessment, progress monitoring, and responsive instructional adjustment through an equity lens.

In pages 47–56, Wehmeyer and Kurth extend the conversation about inclusive education into the domains of assessment, progress monitoring, and sustained instructional adjustment. Inclusion is not complete once students are present in general education settings with embedded supports — it must also include inclusive systems of evaluation that align IEP goals with grade-level standards and use formative assessment to inform ongoing instruction. Assessment should be ongoing, transparent, and collaborative, allowing educators to monitor student growth while maintaining high expectations. Critically, progress monitoring must not isolate students from core instruction but should instead inform how supports are adjusted *within* the general education context. The authors also underscore the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration and family partnership to ensure that instruction remains responsive and culturally sustaining, creating feedback loops that are shaped by knowledge of the whole student — including their cultural background, language context, and identity.

Within the theme of neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, and multiply identified learners, this chapter reinforces that inclusive education requires assessment practices that recognize variability in expression, communication, and processing. Assessment data should be interpreted through a strengths-based lens: patterns of student evidence reveal what supports are working and where the environment is creating friction, not simply who is falling behind. Inclusion becomes dynamic when educators design feedback loops that sustain belonging, agency, and access over time rather than merely documenting deficits.

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- For neurodivergent learners: formative assessment must account for variability in how students demonstrate understanding. A student who cannot complete a timed written response may demonstrate sophisticated thinking through verbal explanation, collaborative building, or iterative revision — and assessment design must make that visible.
- For CLD learners: progress monitoring that does not distinguish between language acquisition and content mastery misreads growth and misattributes difficulty. Culturally sustaining assessment asks what the student knows, not just how fluently they can express it in academic English.
- For students with multiple identities: family partnership and interdisciplinary collaboration are not add-ons — they are the mechanism by which assessment data gains the context needed to be interpreted fairly and used responsively.

**Connection to Post 7:** This reading grounds Post 7's argument that assessment in inclusive classrooms must serve as a feedback mechanism for instructional design, not just a measurement of student performance. When a student's evidence of learning looks different from what a standardized prompt expects, the question is not whether they have learned — it is whether the assessment was designed to see what they know.

## Session 8 — Reading Summary
### *NSTTAC Indicator 13 Checklist*
**Source:** National Secondary Transition Technical Assistance Center (NSTTAC), Indicator 13 Checklist

**Chapter focus:** Transition planning as both a compliance requirement and an equity tool — using the IEP to connect a student’s present schooling to adult life, future education, employment, independence, and self-determination.

The NSTTAC Indicator 13 Checklist focuses on whether a student’s IEP includes the required components for high-quality secondary transition planning. At its core, the checklist asks whether the IEP contains measurable postsecondary goals in education or training, employment, and, when appropriate, independent living; whether those goals are updated annually; whether they are based on age-appropriate transition assessments; and whether the student’s transition services, course of study, and annual IEP goals clearly support movement toward those postsecondary outcomes. The checklist also attends to whether the student was invited to participate in the IEP meeting and whether outside agencies were invited when appropriate, with consent. In that sense, Indicator 13 is not just a paperwork tool. It is a way of checking whether the IEP actually connects the student’s present schooling to adult life, future education, employment, independence, and self-determination.

Through an equity lens, the checklist becomes especially important for neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, and multiply-identified students because transition planning can easily become generic if it is not grounded in the student’s identity, strengths, communication style, family context, and lived goals. A measurable postsecondary goal should not simply reflect what the school assumes is realistic; it should reflect what the student wants, what supports they may need, and how their strengths can be developed over time. Age-appropriate transition assessment also needs to be culturally and linguistically responsive. A student who communicates differently, processes information slowly, uses another language at home, or has limited access to certain career experiences should not be misunderstood as lacking ambition or capacity. The assessment process should create multiple ways for the student to express preferences, interests, skills, and concerns.

The checklist also reinforces that inclusive transition planning is structural. Transition services, annual goals, and courses of study should align with the student’s postsecondary goals so that the IEP is not a disconnected collection of services. For a neurodivergent student, this might mean embedding executive-function, communication, or self-advocacy goals into real academic and career-related routines. For a multilingual or CLD student, it might mean ensuring that family knowledge, language access, and culturally meaningful definitions of adulthood are part of the planning process. For students navigating multiple identities, the checklist reminds educators that future planning must be individualized enough to honor complexity while still maintaining high expectations.

Overall, the NSTTAC Indicator 13 Checklist frames transition planning as both a compliance requirement and an equity tool. If used narrowly, it can become a checkbox exercise. If used thoughtfully, it helps educators ask whether the student’s IEP truly prepares them for life beyond school. For the broader puzzle plan, this connects to the idea that inclusive education should not only support access to today’s lesson but also build a coherent path toward agency, belonging, and future opportunity.

**Key ideas for our theme:**
- For neurodivergent learners: measurable postsecondary goals must grow from the student’s actual strengths, interests, and self-advocacy — not from a narrowed set of presumed outcomes based on diagnostic category. Executive-function, communication, and self-regulation goals should be embedded in transition services, not treated as prerequisites before “real” planning begins.
- For CLD learners: age-appropriate transition assessment must be linguistically accessible and culturally sustaining. Family definitions of adulthood, employment, and independence vary — and the IEP process should make space for those definitions rather than defaulting to dominant-culture assumptions.
- For students with multiple identities: the Indicator 13 requirement that the student be *invited* to their own IEP meeting is a floor, not a ceiling. Meaningful participation means the student has been prepared, supported, and given language and tools to advocate for their own future — not simply present in the room.

**Connection to Post 8:** This reading grounds Post 8’s argument that transition planning, like daily instruction, must be designed with the whole student in mind. Compliance with Indicator 13 is necessary but not sufficient; the deeper question is whether the IEP reflects a genuine, individualized, and forward-looking vision of who the student is and who they are becoming.
