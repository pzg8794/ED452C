# ED452C — Comprehensive Overviews for Transition, Self-Determination, Evidence, SEL, and PBIS Readings

This document preserves the comprehensive reading overviews created during ED452C discussion development. The shared theme across these entries is neurodivergence, culturally and linguistically diverse learners, disability, multilingualism, multiple identities, equity, agency, belonging, and future-facing inclusive design.

---

## Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), pp. 41–46

In pages 41–46, Wehmeyer and Kurth focus on designing effective inclusive supports through intentional scheduling, instructional planning, and Universal Design for Learning. They argue that inclusive education requires structural coordination so that supports are embedded within general education rather than delivered in separate settings. Inclusive schedules should prioritize co-teaching, collaboration, and in-class services so that students receiving specialized instruction remain members of the classroom community. Instructional plans must align IEP goals with grade-level standards, emphasizing continued learning rather than remediation detached from core content. The authors stress that “when to teach” should not become “where to teach,” meaning that skill development and targeted goals should be embedded into natural classroom routines rather than isolated from peers. The section also highlights the importance of technology and UDL in reducing barriers from the outset, providing multiple means of representation, expression, and engagement to accommodate learner variability. Through the lens of neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, and multiply-identified students, this chapter reinforces that inclusion is logistical and systemic: decisions about scheduling, instructional layering, and technology access directly influence whether students experience belonging and sustained participation or subtle forms of separation within supposedly inclusive environments.

## Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), pp. 47–56

In pages 47–56, Wehmeyer and Kurth extend the conversation about inclusive education into the domains of assessment, progress monitoring, and sustained instructional adjustment. The authors emphasize that inclusion is not complete once students are present in general education settings with embedded supports; it must also include inclusive systems of evaluation that align IEP goals with grade-level standards and use formative assessment to inform instruction. They argue that assessment should be ongoing, transparent, and collaborative, allowing educators to monitor student growth while maintaining high expectations. Importantly, progress monitoring must not isolate students from core instruction but should instead inform how supports are adjusted within the general education context. This section also underscores the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration and family partnership to ensure that instruction remains responsive and culturally sustaining. Within the course theme of neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, and multiply-identified learners, this chapter reinforces that inclusive education requires assessment practices that recognize variability in expression, communication, and processing. Inclusion becomes dynamic when educators interpret student evidence through a strengths-based lens and design feedback loops that sustain belonging, agency, and access over time rather than merely documenting deficits.

## NSTTAC Indicator 13 Checklist

The NSTTAC Indicator 13 Checklist focuses on whether a student’s IEP includes the required components for high-quality secondary transition planning. At its core, the checklist asks whether the IEP contains measurable postsecondary goals in education or training, employment, and, when appropriate, independent living; whether those goals are updated annually; whether they are based on age-appropriate transition assessments; and whether the student’s transition services, course of study, and annual IEP goals clearly support movement toward those postsecondary outcomes. The checklist also attends to whether the student was invited to participate in the IEP meeting and whether outside agencies were invited when appropriate, with consent. In that sense, Indicator 13 is not just a paperwork tool. It is a way of checking whether the IEP actually connects the student’s present schooling to adult life, future education, employment, independence, and self-determination.

Through the course equity lens, the checklist becomes especially important for neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, disabled, and multiply-identified students because transition planning can easily become generic if it is not grounded in the student’s identity, strengths, communication style, family context, and lived goals. A measurable postsecondary goal should not simply reflect what the school assumes is realistic; it should reflect what the student wants, what supports they may need, and how their strengths can be developed over time. Age-appropriate transition assessment also needs to be culturally and linguistically responsive. For example, a student who communicates differently, processes information slowly, uses another language at home, or has limited access to certain career experiences should not be misunderstood as lacking ambition or capacity. The assessment process should create multiple ways for the student to express preferences, interests, skills, and concerns.

The checklist also reinforces that inclusive transition planning is structural. Transition services, annual goals, and courses of study should align with the student’s postsecondary goals so that the IEP is not a disconnected collection of services. For a neurodivergent student, this might mean embedding executive-function, communication, or self-advocacy goals into real academic and career-related routines. For a multilingual or CLD student, it might mean ensuring that family knowledge, language access, and culturally meaningful definitions of adulthood are part of the planning process. For students navigating multiple identities, the checklist reminds educators that future planning must be individualized enough to honor complexity while still maintaining high expectations.

Overall, the NSTTAC Indicator 13 Checklist frames transition planning as both a compliance requirement and an equity tool. If used narrowly, it can become a checkbox exercise. If used thoughtfully, it helps educators ask whether the student’s IEP truly prepares them for life beyond school. For the broader puzzle plan, this connects to the idea that inclusive education should not only support access to today’s lesson but also build a coherent path toward agency, belonging, and future opportunity.

## OSERS Guide to Transition

The OSERS Guide to Transition focuses on how schools, families, students, and agencies can support students with disabilities as they move from secondary school into adult life. The guide explains transition as a results-oriented process, not a single meeting or form. Transition planning should be based on the student’s strengths, preferences, interests, and needs, and it should connect school experiences to postsecondary education, vocational training, employment, independent living, adult services, and community participation. In that sense, the guide expands the meaning of special education beyond academic access alone. It asks whether students are being prepared for meaningful adulthood.

Through the course equity lens, this reading matters because transition planning can either expand or limit a student’s future. For neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, disabled, multilingual, and multiply-identified students, transition planning must be more than a generic checklist. A student’s goals should reflect their identity, communication style, family context, cultural expectations, access needs, and actual dreams. If a student needs support with executive functioning, self-advocacy, language access, transportation, employment readiness, or independent living, those needs should not be treated as evidence of low potential. They should be treated as areas where the school, family, and agencies can build meaningful support.

The guide also emphasizes collaboration. Transition planning requires student participation, family engagement, school-based planning, and coordination with outside agencies when appropriate. This is especially important because adulthood does not happen inside one classroom or one school system. Students may need connections to vocational rehabilitation, community services, postsecondary disability supports, employment programs, or independent living resources. When these systems do not communicate, students and families are left to navigate adulthood alone. When they work together, transition becomes a bridge instead of a cliff.

For the broader puzzle plan, the OSERS guide connects to the idea that inclusive education must prepare students not only to access today’s lesson, but to build future agency. Transition planning should help students understand themselves, name their goals, communicate their needs, and move toward adulthood with dignity. Used narrowly, transition planning can become procedural compliance. Used thoughtfully, it becomes one of the clearest examples of strengths-based, identity-centered inclusion.

## Wehmeyer & Shogren (2013), Self-Determination

Wehmeyer and Shogren’s reading frames self-determination as causal agency, meaning that students are not simply participating in school but learning to act as people who can make choices, set goals, monitor outcomes, solve problems, and adjust their actions. The reading emphasizes four major characteristics of self-determined behavior: autonomy, self-regulation, psychological empowerment, and self-realization. Autonomy involves acting based on one’s own preferences and interests rather than only following adult direction. Self-regulation involves planning, monitoring progress, and adjusting behavior in response to feedback. Psychological empowerment means students begin to believe that their actions matter and that they can influence outcomes in their lives. Self-realization means students develop a clearer understanding of their strengths, needs, identities, and limitations without being defined by deficit-based assumptions.

Through the course theme, this reading is important because students who are neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, disabled, multilingual, or navigating multiple identities are often denied real opportunities to practice agency. Adults may make choices for them in the name of support, protection, behavior management, language access, or efficiency. However, if students are always directed, prompted, corrected, or rescued before they can make decisions, they may not develop the very skills schools later expect them to demonstrate during transition planning. Self-determination is not built by telling students to advocate for themselves once they are older. It is built through repeated everyday opportunities to choose, explain, lead, reflect, revise, and experience the consequences of their own decisions in supported environments.

The leadership piece matters because student involvement cannot be symbolic. Students need meaningful roles in shaping their learning, their goals, their supports, and their participation in the classroom community. Leadership does not only look like speaking confidently in front of a group or taking charge during an activity. It can also look like asking a clarifying question, identifying what support is needed, helping a peer, noticing a problem in a project, choosing a different strategy, or explaining why one option works better than another. This is especially important for students whose communication styles, processing speed, cultural norms, or disability-related needs may not match traditional images of leadership.

For the broader puzzle plan and future teaching, this reading connects self-determination to inclusive design. If the classroom is built only around compliance, students may learn to follow directions but not to understand themselves as decision-makers. If the classroom is built around agency, students can practice leadership in ways that are connected to their identities, strengths, and access needs. In that sense, self-determination is not a separate transition skill. It is a daily classroom outcome that must be designed for intentionally.

## Wehmeyer & Kurth (2021), pp. 57–69

In pages 57–69, Wehmeyer and Kurth move the discussion from designing and assessing inclusive supports toward the broader question of evidence: how educators know whether inclusive education is actually working. This section follows naturally from the earlier focus on embedded supports, UDL, progress monitoring, and responsive adjustment. It pushes beyond physical placement by asking whether students are truly accessing instruction, participating meaningfully, demonstrating growth, building relationships, and experiencing belonging. Inclusion cannot be evaluated only by whether students with disabilities are present in general education classrooms. It has to be evaluated by whether the environment makes their competence visible and supports their continued learning.

Through the course theme, this section matters because evidence can easily become narrow or biased. If schools only value fast completion, conventional written responses, compliance, or standardized performance, they may miss the learning of students who communicate differently, process slowly, use multiple languages, rely on alternative formats, or show understanding through collaboration, building, oral explanation, revision, or problem-solving. A strengths-based evidence lens asks not only whether the student performed according to one expected format, but whether the environment gave the student a fair opportunity to show competence.

This section also challenges the assumption that separate settings are automatically better when students need support. If inclusive education is evaluated broadly through academic progress, social participation, peer relationships, access to rigorous curriculum, reduced exclusion, and long-term outcomes, then removal from general education becomes harder to justify as the default solution. The issue is not whether students need support; many do. The issue is whether support is delivered in ways that preserve membership, ambition, and connection to the learning community. For neurodivergent and CLD students especially, separation can reduce access to language-rich environments, peer models, authentic collaboration, and high expectations.

For the broader puzzle plan and future teaching, this reading reinforces that inclusion must be evaluated through multiple forms of evidence. A student’s growth may appear in written work, but it may also appear in how they explain their reasoning, ask for support, revise a project, collaborate with peers, persist through frustration, or participate more confidently over time. If teachers define evidence too narrowly, inclusive education can look unsuccessful even when students are growing. If teachers collect evidence across academic, social, behavioral, and identity-centered dimensions, they can better understand whether the classroom is truly designed for belonging and participation.

## CASEL and PBIS

CASEL and PBIS both frame student support as something that should be designed into the whole learning environment rather than added only after students struggle. CASEL defines social and emotional learning as part of education and human development, emphasizing students’ ability to understand themselves, manage emotions and goals, build relationships, make responsible decisions, and participate in caring communities. Its framework organizes SEL around five connected areas: self-awareness, self-management, social awareness, relationship skills, and responsible decision-making. What matters for the course theme is that CASEL does not present SEL as only individual character development. It connects SEL to classrooms, schools, families, caregivers, and communities, which means student growth is shaped by the environments and relationships around them.

Through an equity lens, CASEL is especially relevant for neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, disabled, multilingual, and multiply-identified students because SEL cannot be culturally neutral. Students’ identities, family contexts, language backgrounds, and communication styles shape how they understand emotions, relationships, conflict, decision-making, and belonging. CASEL’s framework pushes educators to design SEL in ways that recognize personal, cultural, and linguistic assets rather than expecting all students to show self-awareness, leadership, or relationship skills in the same way. This matters because a student who is quiet, cautious, overwhelmed, or still processing language may still be developing strong social and emotional competence, even if it does not look like the dominant classroom expectation.

PBIS complements CASEL by showing how social, emotional, behavioral, academic, and mental health supports can be organized through a tiered schoolwide framework. PBIS emphasizes predictable, positive, equitable, and safe learning environments where students, families, educators, and community members help define valued outcomes. It is not a purchased curriculum or a one-time training; it is a systems-change framework that uses teams, data, practices, outcomes, and equity to support students over time. Tier 1 establishes proactive support for all students by teaching expectations, acknowledging positive behavior, responding instructionally to unwanted behavior, and using data to guide decisions. Tier 2 and Tier 3 add more targeted or individualized supports when students need more than the universal foundation.

Together, CASEL and PBIS reinforce that inclusive education requires both relational and structural design. CASEL helps name the social-emotional competencies students need to understand themselves, connect with others, and act with agency. PBIS helps organize the systems, routines, and data practices that make those supports consistent and equitable. For neurodivergent and CLD students, this pairing is important because behavior, emotion, communication, and identity are often misread when schools rely on narrow expectations. Used thoughtfully, SEL and PBIS can shift the question from “How do we control behavior?” to “How do we build environments where students feel known, supported, respected, and able to participate?”
