\section*{Article and APA Citation}

Rivers, S. E., Brackett, M. A., Reyes, M. R., Elbertson, N. A., \& Salovey, P. (2013). Improving the social and emotional climate of classrooms: A clustered randomized controlled trial testing the RULER Approach. \textit{Prevention Science, 14}(1), 77--87. https://doi.org/10.1007/s11121-012-0305-2

\section*{Summary of Key Information}

This empirical study tested whether the RULER Approach to social and emotional learning (SEL) could improve the observed climate of upper-elementary classrooms in a large urban district. Using a clustered randomized controlled trial, the authors assigned 62 schools either to integrate RULER into fifth- and sixth-grade English language arts classes or to continue using their standard curriculum. Trained observers then rated classroom climate using multi-level models to account for students nested within classrooms and schools.

Compared to control classrooms, RULER classrooms were rated as warmer and more connected in teacher--student relationships, with greater opportunities for student autonomy and leadership. Teachers in RULER schools were also more likely to focus on students' interests and motivations rather than just task completion. The authors argue that these climate changes matter because a more supportive, empowering, and engaging classroom environment is a key mechanism through which SEL can promote positive youth development and buffer risk.

\section*{What I Learned and Why It Matters}

\begin{ReflectionBox}
\textbf{Learning and reflection.} This article helped me see SEL as a setting-level change, not just a set of individual student lessons. RULER altered how teachers and students interacted daily, which then shifted the emotional tone of the classroom. That framing connects strongly to our course discussions about inclusive education as a redesign of environments so that neurodivergent, chronically ill, disabled, and trauma-experienced students do not have to "earn" belonging.
\end{ReflectionBox}

One important takeaway is that the outcomes the authors measured were not only test scores or behavior incidents, but the quality of relationships, autonomy, and teacher responsiveness. For students whose nervous systems are already working hard---for example, students with ADHD, depression, anxiety, chronic illness, or complex trauma histories---a classroom climate that is predictable, emotionally literate, and respect-based is itself an intervention. SEL work like RULER can be a bridge between disability supports and universal design, especially when teachers use emotion language and regulation tools in ways that normalize support for everyone.

The study also pushed me to think about implementation. Even with a strong evidence base, SEL programs can be implemented in shallow ways that become another compliance task. What impressed me here was the focus on infusing RULER into routine academic instruction rather than treating SEL as a once-a-week add-on. As a future educator, I want to pay attention to how I use check-ins, feeling words, and co-created norms so that neurodivergent and culturally and linguistically diverse students are not singled out as the ones who "need" SEL. Instead, SEL should be part of the shared language of the room, making it safer to ask for help, name overwhelm, and negotiate accommodations.

Finally, the article reminded me that classroom climate work has to be paired with structural equity. The sample was a large urban district, and the authors name the potential of RULER to promote youth development. As a practitioner, I need to connect that promise with real issues of racism, ableism, language status, and health access. A warmer climate is necessary but not sufficient if some students are still over-disciplined, under-supported, or expected to self-regulate without adult co-regulation. My goal is to use SEL approaches like RULER to build classrooms where students' multiple identities are recognized and where support is something we design into the environment, not something a student has to apologize for.
