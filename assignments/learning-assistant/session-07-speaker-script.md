# ED452C Learning Assistant Lesson — Speaker Script

**Session:** 7 — Evidence in Support of Inclusive Education  
**Use:** Presenter notes / what to say slide by slide  
**Deck:** `learning-assistant/session-07-evidence-inclusive-education.tex`

---

## Slide 1 — Evidence in Support of Inclusive Education

Say:

Good afternoon everyone. Today I am leading our Session 7 learning assistant lesson on evidence in support of inclusive education.

The main idea I want us to hold onto is this: inclusion is not working just because students are physically in the room. If we want to know whether inclusion is actually working, we have to ask what kind of evidence we are collecting.

For me, the four words on this slide are the anchor: access, participation, growth, and belonging. If our evidence only shows that a student was present or finished a task, we may be missing the deeper question of whether they were able to participate meaningfully and show what they know.

Transition:

So today, I want us to think less like we are checking whether inclusion happened, and more like we are investigating what inclusion made visible.

---

## Slide 2 — Today's purpose

Say:

There are three goals for today. First, we are going to reframe evidence beyond placement and task completion. Second, we are going to think about what gets missed when our evidence is too narrow. Third, we are going to practice designing evidence collection that honors learner variability.

The question at the bottom is important to me: it is not only, did the student finish? It is, what did the environment allow us to see?

That question matters because a student’s performance is never separate from the task, the supports, the language, the pacing, the format, and the relationships around them.

Transition:

Let’s start with the big idea from the reading.

---

## Slide 3 — Big idea from the reading

Say:

The reading pushed me to think about evidence for inclusion as more than physical placement. A student can be in a general education classroom and still be excluded by the way the lesson is designed or by the way learning is measured.

So the evidence we collect should help us ask: Is the student accessing instruction? Are they participating meaningfully? Are they showing growth? Are they still connected to the classroom community?

The quote on the slide says, “A student can be present and still be excluded by what we choose to measure.” That is the main tension for today. If we only measure what is easiest for adults to see, we may confuse access barriers with lack of ability.

Transition:

So if evidence is bigger than task completion, what else can count?

---

## Slide 4 — What can count as evidence?

Say:

When we think of evidence, we often think first of written products, assessments, or completed assignments. Those matter, but they are not the whole picture.

Evidence can also include a student explaining their thinking to a peer, a quick teacher conference, a revision after feedback, a strategy shift, a question a student asks when they are stuck, or a change in confidence and participation over time.

This is especially important in inclusive classrooms because not every student shows understanding in the same format or at the same speed. Sometimes the first visible product is not the strongest evidence of learning.

Transition:

That brings us to the equity question: who gets misread when our evidence is too narrow?

---

## Slide 5 — Equity lens: who gets misread?

Say:

When evidence is narrow, we can accidentally measure who had the easiest access to the task instead of who understood the content.

This matters for students whose processing, language, communication, sensory needs, or cultural expectations do not match the classroom’s default rhythm. A student may need more processing time, a different way to respond, a clearer entry point, or a chance to talk through an idea before writing it.

If we only look for the fastest answer, the cleanest written response, or the most visible participation, we may miss the students who are thinking deeply but showing it differently.

The line I want us to sit with is: if we only measure the easiest-to-see behavior, we may miss the hardest-working thinking.

Transition:

Now we are going to try that idea with a short activity.

---

## Slide 6 — Activity 1: Evidence sort

Say:

For this first activity, pair up or form a group of three. I want you to sort the examples on the slide into three categories: easy-to-measure evidence, process evidence, and evidence we often miss.

The examples are: finished worksheet, explained to a peer, revised design, asked for new wording, and joined discussion.

Take about two minutes to sort them. Then discuss this question: Which students are most likely to be misread if we only use the easy-to-measure evidence?

Pause for activity.

After discussion, say:

What I want to highlight is that none of these evidence types are automatically wrong. The issue is whether we rely on only one kind. If the only thing we count is the finished worksheet, then we may miss the student who explained the concept beautifully to a peer, or the student who revised their thinking after feedback.

Transition:

Now let’s move from sorting evidence to redesigning how we collect it.

---

## Slide 7 — Activity 2: Redesign the evidence plan

Say:

For the second activity, think of one lesson you teach, have taught, or remember as a student. It can be a STEM lesson, reading lesson, discussion, project, or anything else.

Your task is to add three things to the evidence plan. First, add two process checkpoints. Second, add one student voice prompt. Third, add one alternative way for students to show understanding.

A process checkpoint could be a quick pause where students explain their next step. A student voice prompt could be, “What helped you enter the task?” or “What is still blocking you?” An alternative way to show understanding could be drawing, speaking, building, annotating, demonstrating, or explaining to a partner.

The question is: what would you see now that you might have missed before?

Pause for activity.

After discussion, say:

The point is not to lower the goal. The point is to give the goal more ways to become visible. A stronger evidence plan helps teachers see learning across the process, not only at the final product.

Transition:

Let’s close by turning this into a teacher takeaway.

---

## Slide 8 — Leave with this

Say:

The takeaway I want to leave us with is simple: look again before you conclude.

Before we conclude that a student did not learn, we should ask: Did the student have access to the task? Did we collect evidence across the learning process? Did our evidence plan value belonging and participation, not just completion?

For me, this connects directly to inclusive teaching because evidence is not neutral. What we choose to count shapes what we believe students can do. If our evidence is too narrow, our conclusions about students will be too narrow too.

So as future teachers, we have to design evidence systems that let more students be seen accurately. Inclusion is working when students can show what they know, participate with dignity, and remain connected to the learning community.

Closing line:

Thank you. My final question for us is: how can we collect evidence of inclusion that captures belonging, participation, and growth without reducing students to test scores, task completion, or compliance?

---

## Optional timing guide

For a 10-minute lesson, use about one minute per slide and keep each activity to one minute.

For a 15-minute lesson, use about one minute per explanation slide and two to three minutes per activity.

For a 20-minute lesson, allow more discussion after each activity and invite one or two groups to share out.
