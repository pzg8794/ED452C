# Session 3 — Discussion Post

**Course:** ED452C  
**Theme:** Person-Environment Fit and the Launch Routine as Diagnostic Tool

---

While reading Wehmeyer and Kurth, I kept coming back to the idea that inclusion is not something we add after a student struggles. It is something built into the way we design participation from the very first move of a lesson. When a student is neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, disabled, or navigating multiple identities at once, the difficulty may not originate in the student. It may come from how the environment defines entry, competence, and success. I saw this clearly during the Pine Brook IGNITE STEM model airplane project with squishy circuits. On the surface, the task looked universally engaging: build, test, redesign. But in practice, students did not enter the work on equal footing. Some moved quickly into wing design and motor placement. Others hesitated—not because they lacked ideas, but because they were still decoding the warm-up, figuring out how to handle unfamiliar materials, negotiating roles within a group of three, or trying to make sense of multi-step build directions delivered quickly. In those moments, it would have been easy to interpret slower starts as lower competence. A strengths-based lens forced a different question: What thinking is already present, and what part of the launch routine is blocking it from becoming visible?

This is where identity becomes instructional. A hands-on STEM task can quietly assume a “default” participant: someone comfortable with materials, fluent in school-language directions, able to filter background noise, quick to process multi-step verbal explanations, and confident in taking risks in front of peers. For a multilingual learner, a neurodivergent student, or a student whose prior experiences with engineering tasks differ from what school expects, those assumptions create invisible friction. If I fail to notice that friction, I risk mislabeling unfamiliarity, sensory load, or executive-function demand as inability. I saw this at Pine Brook during an AI brainstorming lesson. The task asked students to pick something they cared about and use an idea generator to build a project topic. What I noticed was that “stuck” looked the same whether a student was frozen by a login error, overwhelmed by too many AI-generated options, or unsure what “connecting to computer science” even meant. The surface behavior was identical. The barrier was not. Designing for that variability means the launch has to slow down long enough to let the teacher tell the difference — not assume it.

**My question is:** When a student’s barrier only becomes visible after the lesson is already in motion, what does it mean to have designed a lesson for inclusion in the first place — and at what point does a launch routine become a diagnostic tool rather than just an entry point?
