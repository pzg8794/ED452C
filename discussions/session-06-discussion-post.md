# Session 6 — Discussion Post

**Course:** ED452C  
**Theme:** Inclusion as Structural Design — Embedded Supports, Scheduling, and UDL Beyond the Lesson

---

Wehmeyer and Kurth pushed me to think about inclusion as a structural design question, not just an instructional attitude. These pages emphasize that inclusive support should be embedded into schedules, instructional plans, and daily routines rather than delivered separately. When support is layered into general education—through co-teaching, embedded IEP goals, and Universal Design for Learning—students remain members of the classroom community while still receiving individualized support.

This connects directly to my Pine Brook teaching placement. In reviewing transcripts from small-group and whole-class STEM and technology lessons, I noticed how often support was happening informally during transitions, partner talk, or material distribution. For example, moments like re-explaining directions while handing out materials, clarifying vocabulary during group work, or prompting a student quietly before public sharing functioned as embedded instruction. Those supports did not require removing a student from the classroom. They were layered into the routine.

What stands out to me from the reading is the warning that “when to teach” should not become “where to teach.” If a neurodivergent student needs practice with communication goals or executive-function skills, those opportunities can exist inside science discussions, collaborative build sessions, or transition routines. A multilingual student’s language development can be supported within content learning, not outside of it. When instruction is embedded, support becomes normalized rather than stigmatized.

The UDL and technology section also complicates how I think about equity. Technology is not inclusive by default. It becomes inclusive when it reduces barriers from the beginning. Providing text in multiple formats, using speech-to-text, offering structured templates, or allowing varied demonstration formats helps ensure that learning expectations remain high while access routes multiply. That matters especially for students navigating neurodivergence, language differences, and sensory or processing variability.

For my broader puzzle plan, this reading reinforces that inclusive design is logistical, not just philosophical. Scheduling decisions, grouping structures, instructional layering, and format flexibility determine whether students stay in the learning community or are quietly separated from it.

My question is: How can teachers intentionally design daily routines so that embedded supports are visible, normalized, and proactive rather than reactive accommodations that only appear after a student struggles?
