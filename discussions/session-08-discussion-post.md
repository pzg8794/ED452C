# Session 8 — Discussion Post

**Course:** ED452C  
**Theme:** Transition Planning as Equity — NSTTAC Indicator 13 and Identity-Centered IEP Design

---

The NSTTAC Indicator 13 Checklist made me think about transition planning as more than a compliance requirement. On the surface, the checklist asks whether the IEP includes measurable postsecondary goals, transition assessments, transition services, a course of study, annual goals, student participation, and agency involvement when appropriate. But I see a deeper question: Does the student’s education actually connect to the life they are trying to build after school?

This matters especially for neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, disabled, and multiply-identified students because transition planning can easily become generic if educators only focus on completing the required sections. A measurable postsecondary goal should not simply reflect what the school thinks is realistic. It should reflect the student’s strengths, communication style, family context, identity, interests, and future possibilities. If a student processes differently, communicates differently, uses another language at home, or has had limited access to certain career experiences, that should not be mistaken for lack of ambition or ability.

The checklist also helped me think about my teaching placement. Even though my placement was with younger students, I saw how early ideas about agency, self-advocacy, and future readiness begin. When students chose project ideas, explained their thinking, negotiated roles, or reflected on what helped them participate, they were practicing skills that later become part of transition planning. In that sense, transition does not suddenly begin in high school. The habits that support meaningful transition; choice-making, communication, self-awareness, goal-setting, and access to support, are built much earlier through everyday classroom routines.

For my broader puzzle plan (research) and future teaching, Indicator 13 connects to the idea that inclusive education should also help build a coherent path toward agency, belonging, and future opportunity. If the checklist is used narrowly, it can become paperwork. If it is used thoughtfully, it becomes a tool for asking whether the student’s IEP truly honors who they are and where they want to go.

**My question:** How can teachers and IEP teams use transition checklists like Indicator 13 as tools for student agency and identity-centered planning, rather than treating them as paperwork to complete for compliance?
