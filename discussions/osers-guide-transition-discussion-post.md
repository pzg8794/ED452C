# OSERS Guide to Transition — Discussion Post

**Course:** ED452C  
**Theme:** Transition Planning as Coordinated, Identity-Centered Support

---

The OSERS Guide to Transition made me think about transition planning as one of the clearest tests of whether inclusion is truly strengths-based. It is not enough for a student to receive services during school if those services do not connect to the life the student is trying to build after school. The guide frames transition as a results-oriented process based on the student’s strengths, preferences, interests, and needs. Transition planning should not simply ask, “What supports does this student require?” It should also ask, “What future is this student moving toward, and how are we helping them get there?”

This connects to the broader equity themes we have been tracing throughout this course. For neurodivergent, culturally and linguistically diverse, disabled, multilingual, and multiply-identified students, future planning can become limited if educators confuse support needs with limited potential. A student who needs help with executive functioning, communication, transportation, language access, or self-advocacy may still have ambitious goals for education, employment, independent living, and community participation. The role of the IEP team should be to build the bridge between those goals and the support needed to reach them. This also connects back to my Indicator 13 reflection: transition planning should be about student agency, not just compliance. The OSERS guide adds that agency depends on coordinated systems, so teachers need to notice students’ preferences, barriers, and communication styles early enough for future planning to reflect who they actually are.

For my broader puzzle plan (research) and future teaching, this reading reinforces that inclusive education should be future-facing. Access to the current lesson matters, but inclusion should also help students build agency over time. If transition planning is treated only as compliance, it can become paperwork. If it is treated as identity-centered planning, it can help students imagine and move toward a future that reflects who they are and what they want.

My question is: How can teachers help students practice self-determination and future planning throughout everyday instruction, instead of waiting until transition planning formally begins?
