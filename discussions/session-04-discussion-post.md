# Session 4 — Discussion Post

**Course:** ED452C  
**Theme:** Strengths-Based Design, Self-Determination, and the Right to Full Participation

---

Wehmeyer and Kurth's argument that disability is "essentially irrelevant" when a student is fully participating stopped me in my tracks. Not because it minimizes what students carry, but because it moves the teacher's first question away from "How disabled is this student?" and toward "What is blocking full participation right now, and what is mine to change?" That shift is not a small rhetorical move. It is a completely different starting position for lesson design, and it has real consequences for what a teacher notices, what they prioritize, and who gets treated as a capable thinker from the first moment of class.

I saw what full participation — and its absence — actually look like during the Lego sail car construction lesson at Pine Brook IGNITE. The task was genuinely engaging: working in small groups, students followed a visual build guide to assemble a Lego sail car from a sorted set of pieces, then tested it. The structure had clear logic. Page 62. Sort your pieces into the tray before you build. Only take what you need. Color-code everything back into the bin at the end. The teacher circled the room, narrated what he was looking for, connected effort to a sticker reward, and offered hands-on support at each table. On the surface, it looked inclusive because every student had a tray, a task, a role, and a shot at winning.

But watching it closely, the design made some things invisible. The directions were multi-step, fast, and almost entirely verbal — announced in rapid sequence while students were simultaneously managing physical materials, negotiating with group members, and tracking the competitive clock. Students who needed a moment to decode the visual on page 62 before touching pieces were already behind by the time the instruction shifted. At one table, a conflict broke out between two students — one had been directing their partner loudly, the partner shut down and needed a break. The teacher handled it with care, but the structure of the task had created a dynamic where speed and assertiveness were rewarded and hesitation read as failure. A quieter student, a student processing the directions more slowly, or a student whose prior Lego experience differed from what the task assumed — all of them were operating at a disadvantage that the sticker system made visible but the lesson design had not addressed. Notably, after class the lead teacher reflected that the real richness of the task — the redesign phase, where students could modify their sail car and test the changes — never happened because the initial build took the whole period. The part of the lesson most connected to student agency and self-determination had been designed as optional, and it was the first thing dropped.

That is the lesson Session 4 helped me name. Wehmeyer and Kurth describe strengths-based approaches as beginning with what a student already knows and can do, and then asking what supports are needed to make full participation possible in the preferred environment. UDL operationalizes this by designing materials so that variability — in language, processing speed, prior experience, sensory need, social confidence — is the expected condition, not the exception. But the Lego sail car lesson showed me that task design can signal strengths-based values without actually structuring them in. Sorting pieces before building is a good scaffold. A visual guide is a good scaffold. But if the only way into the task is through fast verbal multi-step directions, if competition is the primary motivator, and if the part of the lesson that asks students to make decisions and test their own ideas is treated as a bonus rather than the core, then the design is still centering the most compliant, fastest-processing learner in the room and calling it universal.

Self-determination is not a reward for finishing. It is, as Wehmeyer and Kurth argue, what we are designing toward. For a neurodivergent student whose thinking becomes visible when they get to test and revise rather than follow and reproduce, cutting the redesign phase is not a neutral time management decision. For a multilingual learner whose confidence emerges when they can show spatial reasoning without language load, a task anchored in rapid verbal directions is not giving them a fair look at what they understand. For any student navigating multiple identities in a room where speed and loudness are implicitly being rewarded, the question of whether they belong in the winner circle is already being answered by the design before the clock even starts.

**My question is:** When the richest part of a lesson — the part where students exercise agency, test their own ideas, and build self-determination — is chronically the part that gets cut for time, what does that tell us about how we have actually ordered our priorities, and how do we redesign tasks so that student decision-making is the non-negotiable core rather than the extension?
