# Session 4 — Discussion Post

**Course:** ED452C  
**Theme:** Strengths-Based Design, Self-Determination, and the Right to Full Participation

---

Wehmeyer and Kurth's argument that disability becomes "essentially irrelevant" when environments are designed for full participation shifts the teacher's first question away from "What is wrong with this student?" and toward "What in this design is blocking full participation?" That changes what a teacher notices and who gets treated as a capable thinker from the first moment of class.

I saw this during the Lego sail car construction lesson at Pine Brook IGNITE. The task had a clear structure: sort pieces into your tray from page 62, build with your team, test the car, and clean up by color. On the surface, it looked inclusive. Every student had a tray, a role, and an incentive to complete the build. But the directions were fast, multi-step, and almost entirely verbal, delivered while students were already handling materials and managing group dynamics. Students who needed a moment to decode the visual guide before touching pieces were already behind. At one table, a student who had been directing her partner too loudly triggered a conflict, and the partner shut down. The teacher handled it well, but the design had rewarded speed and assertiveness, leaving hesitation looking like failure. After class, the lead teacher noted that the redesign phase, where students could modify and retest their car, never happened because the build took the whole period. The part of the lesson most tied to agency and self-determination was treated as optional, and it was the first thing cut.

That is what Session 4 helped me name. UDL means designing for variability as the expected condition. But the sail car lesson showed me that a task can appear strengths-based without actually protecting the conditions that make student agency possible. Sorting pieces is a good scaffold. A visual guide is a good scaffold. But if the only entry point is rapid verbal directions, if competition is the core motivator, and if student decision-making is always the bonus that gets dropped, then the design is still centering the fastest, most verbally fluent learner and calling it universal. Self-determination is not a reward for finishing; it is what we are supposed to be designing toward.

**My question is:** When the part of the lesson where students exercise agency is the first thing cut for time, what does that reveal about how we have actually ordered our priorities? How do we redesign so that student decision-making becomes the non-negotiable core rather than the optional extension?
