# Session 4 — Discussion Post

**Course:** ED452C  
**Theme:** Strengths-Based Design, Self-Determination, and the Right to Full Participation

---

Wehmeyer and Kurth's argument that disability is "essentially irrelevant" when a student is fully participating shifts the teacher's first question away from "What is wrong with this student?" and toward "What in this design is blocking full participation?" That is not a small rhetorical move. It changes what a teacher notices and who gets treated as a capable thinker from the first moment of class.

I saw this during the Lego sail car construction lesson at Pine Brook IGNITE. The task had a clear structure — sort pieces into your tray from page 62, build with your team, test the car, clean up by color. On the surface it looked inclusive: every student had a tray, a role, and a shot at the sticker. But the directions were fast, multi-step, and almost entirely verbal, delivered while students were already handling materials and managing group dynamics. Students who needed a moment to decode the visual guide before touching pieces were already behind. At one table, a student who had been directing her partner too loudly triggered a conflict; the partner shut down. The teacher handled it well, but the design had rewarded speed and assertiveness and left hesitation looking like failure. After class, the lead teacher noted that the redesign phase — where students could modify and retest their car — never happened because the build took the whole period. The part of the lesson most tied to agency and self-determination was treated as optional, and it was the first thing cut.

That is what Session 4 helped me name. UDL means designing for variability as the expected condition, not the exception. But the sail car lesson showed that a task can signal strengths-based values without actually building them in. Sorting pieces is a good scaffold. A visual guide is a good scaffold. But if the only entry point is rapid verbal directions, if competition is the core motivator, and if student decision-making is always the bonus that gets dropped, then the design is still centering the fastest, most verbally fluent learner and calling it universal. Self-determination is not a reward for finishing — it is what we are supposed to be designing toward.

**My question is:** When the part of the lesson where students exercise agency is chronically the first thing cut for time, what does that reveal about how we have actually ordered our priorities — and how do we redesign so that student decision-making is the non-negotiable core?
