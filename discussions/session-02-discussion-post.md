# Session 2 Discussion Post

One idea that stood out to me from Wehmeyer and Kurth is the shift from viewing disability through a deficit lens to planning instruction from a strengths-based lens. The reading pushes me to think about inclusion as more than placement in the same classroom. A student can be physically present in a general education space and still be excluded from the real learning if the task, language, routines, tools, or assessment only work for a narrow kind of learner.

This connects directly to my Pine Brook IGNITE placement. In the Grade 4 Minecraft Education work, students brought very different strengths and access needs. Some students had strong computational ideas but needed support with sign-in, keyboard fluency, reading on-screen directions, following multi-step routines, managing frustration, or navigating an open-ended digital environment. In that setting, a student could look like they were struggling with coding when the real barrier was startup friction, language load, or interface navigation. That changes the teacher's responsibility. The inclusive move is not to lower the goal; it is to design the route into the goal more carefully so students can actually show their thinking.

I saw the same idea in my AI-supported learning work from EDU442. Students need routines that protect their thinking and authorship while also giving them access to planning, revision, and language support. The routine Ask -> Check -> Choose -> Change -> Explain is useful because it does not treat the tool as an answer machine. It gives students a structure for making choices, explaining those choices, and staying visible as thinkers.

For me, the reading's goal is connected to the larger puzzle plan for my portfolio. Each course artifact should become one piece of evidence showing how I design for students' strengths before they fail, not only after a problem appears. My question is: How can teachers build strong Tier 1 routines that support everyone while still noticing the individual strengths, identities, and access needs that should shape instruction for particular students?
