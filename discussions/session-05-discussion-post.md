# Session 5 — Discussion Post

**Course:** ED452C  
**Theme:** Self-Determination as Causal Agency — Designing for the Student Who Decides

---

Wehmeyer and Shogren's central claim is deceptively simple: self-determination is not a personality trait and not a transition-planning milestone reserved for older students. It is the cumulative product of everyday experiences of choosing, acting, and adjusting — and students who are chronically denied those experiences do not arrive underprepared because of their disability, their language background, or their identity. They arrive underprepared because their learning environments never treated their agency as the goal. The four characteristics Wehmeyer and Shogren identify — autonomy, self-regulation, psychological empowerment, and self-realization — are not outcomes of compliant schooling. They are outcomes of classrooms that consistently ask students to be the causal agent: the one who sets the direction, reads the result, and decides what comes next.

I saw this most clearly during the AI project brainstorming lesson I taught at Pine Brook IGNITE. The design of the lesson asked students to use an AI idea generator to identify a personal interest, generate a computer science project topic around that interest, and then build a planning guide — all in one class period. What struck me was not the technology. It was the moment when students had to choose. A student who had been quiet for most of the setup phase suddenly came alive when the prompt said: *give me four computer science project ideas for grade four and five* with his interest in the blank. He chose Minecraft. When the first batch of ideas came back too complex, he asked the AI to simplify without being told to — he read the output, made a judgment, and adjusted the direction. That is autonomy and self-regulation happening in real time, in a fourth-grade classroom, from a student who had not raised his hand once during the verbal instructions. What the task did differently was build the choice point directly into the structure: not as a reward, not as an extension, but as the first move. The student did not have to earn the right to have a preference. The preference was the entry point. For neurodivergent learners, CLD students, and any student whose full capacity has been consistently underread by adult-directed environments, that structural shift — from compliance-first to agency-first — is not an enrichment adjustment. It is the difference between schooling that builds self-determination and schooling that spends years systematically preventing it.

**My question is:** If self-determination develops through cumulative experience of causal agency across the entire K–12 span, how do we audit our lessons not just for access — can every student reach the task? — but for agency: does every student get to be the one who decides? And for students whose identities, neurotypes, or language backgrounds have led us to over-scaffold and over-direct, what do we owe them in terms of deliberately rebuilding those missed experiences of causal agency?
